A Radiologist's view of Brain Tumors

Brain Tumors - Uncontrolled, unnatural growth and division of brain tissue - are thankfully not very common but they are one of the most lethal cancers. A Radiologist's job is to diagnose and analyse these tumours in order to provide the best treatment plan. Currently the time from scan results to report can take in excess of four solid hours REFNEEDED and, due to the requirement of highly specialised trained individuals, these are done back-to-back reducing accuracy.

Primary & Metastatic

In primary brain tumors, the origin of the cells are brain tissue which then become cancerous cells.
In metastatic tumors, cells become cancerous at any part of the body and spread into the brain.
Most tumors that begin in the brain do not spread meastatically

Gliomas

The majority of primary brain tumors originate from glial cells (hence called Glioma) and are classified using the World Health Organisation (WHO) system [1] into low-grade glioma (LGG) (grades I and II) and high-grade glioma (grade II anaplastic glioma and grade IV glioblastoma).
  • Grade I: The tissue is benign. The cells look nearly like normal brain cells and cell growth is slow.
  • Grade II: The tissue is malignant. The cells look less like normal cells than in grade I.
  • Grade III: Abnormal cells are actively growing.
  • Grade IV: Malignant tissue is most abnormal and very aggressive.

High Grade

Cases each year (US)1: 12,000
Median Survival: 14.6 months
Five-year surivival rate: < 10%

High-grade malignant brain tumors generally lead to short life expectancy and limited treatment. The aggressive nature of this illness necessitates efficient diagnosis and treatment plans to improve quality of and extend patient life. Automatic segmentation methods that are fast and reliable in clinical settings are vital for these tasks. Algorithmic segmentation is a particularly challenging task.
